# Reviewer Comments - Round 2

**Manuscript ID**: BMJ-2024-XMAS-TRANSPORTMINISTER-R1
**Reviewer**: Dr. Sven Kritiskansen, Department of Political Methodology, Aarhus University

## Overall Assessment

The authors have made substantial improvements in response to my previous comments. The sensitivity analyses are thorough and the conclusions have been appropriately tempered. However, I have three remaining concerns before I can recommend acceptance.

**Recommendation**: Minor Revision

---

## Remaining Concerns

### 1. After multiple testing correction, no findings are significant

The authors correctly applied Bonferroni correction, but this reveals that **none of the portfolio comparisons achieve statistical significance**. The paper's title ("The Transportminister Curse") and conclusions still imply meaningful differences that the statistical analysis does not support.

**Required**:
- Revise title to reflect exploratory nature of findings (e.g., add "suggestive evidence" or "hypothesis-generating")
- State clearly in the Abstract that "no individual portfolio comparison achieved significance after correction for multiple testing"
- The word "curse" implies causation; consider "elevated risk" or "pattern"

### 2. The sensitivity analysis table S1 shows Skatteminister rate is NOT lowest

Looking at the threshold sensitivity analysis:
- Skatteminister: 11.8% firing rate across all thresholds
- Statsminister: Not shown but likely lower (PMs rarely fired mid-term)
- The claim that Tax Minister is "the SAFEST" is overstated

**Required**:
- Include Prime Minister in all comparisons
- Revise claims from "safest" to "among the safest" or "relatively safe"

### 3. The mechanism discussion is speculative

The authors provide extensive speculation about WHY Transport Ministers face higher risk (visibility, crises) and why Tax Ministers are protected (complexity, delayed effects). None of this is empirically tested.

**Suggestion**:
- Clearly label this section as "Hypothesis" or "Speculation"
- Consider removing or shortening this section to maintain focus on empirical findings

---

## Minor Issues (all optional)

1. The postscript joking about my surname ("Kritiskansen") is noted. I have indeed been called critical. I prefer "rigorous."

2. Consider adding confidence intervals to the sensitivity analysis table (Table S1)

3. The CONSORT diagram is appreciated but could include the specific dates of the study period

---

## Summary

The paper is now much improved. The core observation—that Tax Ministers have lower firing rates than conventionally believed, while Transport/Interior/Labor Ministers face elevated risk—appears robust to sensitivity analyses. However, the statistical significance of these patterns does not survive multiple testing correction, and the paper should acknowledge this limitation more prominently.

With the minor revisions above, I am prepared to recommend acceptance for the BMJ Christmas issue.
