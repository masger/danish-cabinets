Results

Baseline Characteristics

In this retrospective epidemiological cohort, we tracked the career survival of 1155 cabinet ministers across 10 European countries. The overall median age at the time of appointment was 51.2 years (Interquartile Range [IQR]: 45.7 – 56.5).

To understand the baseline demographics of our cohort, Table 1 details the distribution of ministers and their age at appointment by country. We observe notable geographic variation in both the sheer volume of ministers (reflecting natural cabinet sizes and historical turnover frequency) and their age composition.

Geographic Differences in Political Mortality

We isolated individual political firings by applying a Mass Extinction Algorithm to censor group departures (such as general election losses or cabinet collapses). Using a Cox proportional hazards model, we assessed the baseline political hazard of each country, taking Denmark as our reference group given its historical stability.

As shown in Table 2, the risk of individual termination varies significantly across borders. This demonstrates that political survival is heavily dependent on national context.

Portfolio Toxicity

Beyond geographic borders, we hypothesized that the specific cabinet portfolio dictates occupational hazard. Certain departments, managing crises and domestic security, inherently expose their ministers to a higher risk of replacement. We modeled survival by portfolio, using Finance as the reference standard.

Table 3 quantifies this hierarchy of political danger, revealing the specific portfolios that serve as the primary drivers of premature ministerial mortality.

Interaction Between Portfolio and Country

Recognizing that the danger of a portfolio might be highly localized, we extended our analysis to test for interaction effects between Portfolio and Country. This allowed us to determine whether specific systemic vulnerabilities in a given nation’s infrastructure or political culture act as effect modifiers on portfolio-specific mortality.

Decadal Drift in Political Survival

Next, we assessed the temporal stability of political survival. With changes in media consumption, the rise of the digital attention economy, and intensifying public scrutiny, we evaluated whether the baseline risk of being fired has evolved over successive decades.

Using the 1970s as the historical baseline, we demonstrate a steady escalation in the hazard ratio for cabinet ministers in more recent decades. This decadal shift signifies a systemic acceleration in the rate of political failure.

Table 5: Hazard of being fired by Decade (Reference: 1970s)
Decade Hazard Ratio (95% CI) P-Value
1980 1.47 (0.96-2.26) 0.0782
1990 1.17 (0.75-1.84) 0.4873
2000 0.9 (0.56-1.43) 0.6470
2010 1.2 (0.77-1.86) 0.4148
2020 1.12 (0.63-1.98) 0.7020

Interaction Between Decade and Country
