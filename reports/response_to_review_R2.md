## REMAINING CONCERNS ADDRESSED

### 1. Title and conclusions should reflect exploratory nature

**Reviewer concern**: The title implies causation and significance that the data do not support after multiple testing correction.

**Response**: We fully accept this criticism. The title has been revised to:

> **"SKATTEMINISTER SYNDROME RECONSIDERED: Exploratory evidence that Transport, not Tax, may be the cursed portfolio in Danish government"**

The Abstract now states:

> "After correction for multiple testing, no individual portfolio comparison achieved statistical significance (Bonferroni-adjusted p > 0.05 for all comparisons). However, the consistent direction of effects—with Tax Ministers showing the lowest firing rate (11.8%) and Transport/Interior/Labor Ministers showing elevated rates (33-56%)—suggests that conventional wisdom about the 'Skatteminister Syndrome' deserves reconsideration."

We have replaced "curse" with "elevated risk" throughout, except in the discussion of folk beliefs where "curse" is the term used in popular discourse.

**Changes made**: Title revised; Abstract revised; terminology tempered throughout.

---

### 2. Skatteminister is not the LOWEST—include Statsminister

**Reviewer concern**: Prime Ministers are rarely fired mid-term; claiming Tax is "the safest" ignores this.

**Response**: We appreciate this correction. Including Statsminister in comparisons:

| Portfolio | Firing Rate |
|-----------|-------------|
| Statsminister | 8.1% |
| Skatteminister | 11.8% |
| Udenrigsminister | 13.2% |
| Finansminister | 21.6% |
| ... | ... |
| Transportminister | 46.7% |
| Indenrigsminister | 55.6% |

The text now reads:

> "Tax Ministers demonstrated among the lowest firing rates (11.8%), second only to Prime Ministers (8.1%), who are by definition protected from dismissal by virtue of leading the government."

We note that Prime Ministers face a different form of political mortality: they can only "die" when the entire government falls. Comparing them to other ministers is therefore somewhat apples-to-oranges, but we include them for completeness.

**Changes made**: Statsminister included in all tables; claims revised from "safest" to "among the safest."

---

### 3. Mechanism discussion is speculative

**Reviewer concern**: The discussion of WHY certain portfolios are dangerous is not empirically tested.

**Response**: We have restructured the Discussion to clearly separate:

1. **Empirical findings** (what we observed)
2. **Hypotheses for future research** (why this might occur)

The relevant section now reads:

> **Hypotheses for Future Research**
>
> We offer the following speculations—explicitly untested—as potential explanations for the observed patterns:
>
> *Why might Transport/Interior portfolios be hazardous?*
> - Infrastructure and crisis management failures are immediately visible
> - Affected constituents (commuters, disaster victims) are vocal
> - Measurable metrics (delays, response times) invite criticism
>
> *Why might Tax portfolios be relatively protected?*
> - Technical complexity makes replacement costly
> - Effects phase in over years, beyond immediate political horizons
> - Diffuse impacts lack concentrated opposition
>
> These hypotheses require empirical testing through qualitative analysis of dismissal circumstances, media coverage analysis, and comparative international research.

**Changes made**: Discussion restructured; speculation clearly labeled.

## CONCLUDING REMARKS

We believe the manuscript now accurately represents both the interesting patterns observed and the limitations of inferring causation from observational data with modest sample sizes.

The core contribution remains: Danish political folklore about the "Skatteminister Syndrome" is not supported by evidence. If anything, the data suggest the opposite pattern. We hope this hypothesis-generating work inspires future researchers to investigate ministerial survival with larger international datasets and more granular outcome data.

We thank Dr. Kritiskansen for his rigorous (not critical!) review, which has substantially improved the manuscript.
